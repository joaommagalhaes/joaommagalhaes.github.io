    Five-nines platform UCaaS Platform Real-time · Cloud
    Angular TypeScript Real-time Cloud
    Read the case study→
    Problem

    Cloud-communications dashboards live on real-time data — presence, calls, queues, analytics. Thousands of events per minute, and every one of them wants to repaint the screen.

    Constraints

    An enterprise UCaaS platform with a 99.999% uptime culture: the frontend must stay responsive under sustained event load, on long-lived sessions that are never refreshed.

    Decision

    Discipline at the subscription boundary: centralised socket subscription management, OnPush change detection with explicit update paths, virtualised lists for high-churn views, and back-pressure — coalescing event bursts before they ever reach the render loop.

    Outcome

    Interfaces that stay smooth while the data never stops. The habits it enforced — measure first, render less, trust the profiler — now shape how I review every frontend change.

    1.5M+ MAU eSIM · Plan Swaps · Campaigns
    React Native ReactJS TypeScript Node.js
    Read the case study→
    Problem

    Every marketing campaign shipped as app code. Each one needed engineering time, a release cycle and app-store review — marketing moved at the speed of the slowest deploy.

    Constraints

    1.5M+ monthly active users, release cadence measured in weeks, campaign authors with no engineering background, and zero room for regressions in critical purchase journeys.

    Decision

    A modular campaign engine in React: a campaign became a declarative configuration, rendered by a registry of plug-and-play blocks — banners, flows, forms, rewards. A new campaign meant a new config, validated and delivered server-side. Not a new app.

    Outcome

    Non-technical teams launched campaigns autonomously. Time-to-market dropped from release-cycle weeks to same-day, and the engine outlived my time on the team as the campaign platform.

    My Approach

    I use AI as a core production methodology, not just an experimental toy. I understand how language models reason, where their context inevitably degrades, and how to design agent flows that remain cohesive across tool sequences and memory boundaries. When I design skills and prompts, I ensure they hold up under genuine user load. The philosophy is simple: remain fast by design, but deliberate by discipline.

    Read the case study→
    Problem

    Family finances lived across spreadsheets, bank exports and paper contracts — no single view, and no easy answers to questions like “what does this contract actually commit us to?”

    Constraints

    Self-hosted and privacy-first: family financial data stays under my control. Built and maintained by one person, so every moving part has to earn its complexity.

    Decision

    A self-hosted budget and wealth platform with an LLM layer on top: documents and contracts are ingested and retrievable through RAG, and agent skills automate the recurring work — categorising transactions, tracking loans, flagging contract deadlines.

    Outcome

    Running in production for my family’s real finances — the strictest user base I know. It’s where I test how far LLMs, RAG and skill design can be pushed before they break.

    This website — under the hood
    
            view source ↗
          
    
          
    The background is one particle system: 9,000 points in a single buffer, drawn in one call. Every section precomputes a formation — a target position and colour per particle. Scrolling picks the two nearest formations and blends between them with a smoothstep; one lerp loop pulls every particle toward its target each frame. No per-frame allocation, no DOM work, one draw call.
    
          
          
    The name at the top is the same trick: the text is rasterised to an offscreen canvas once, and its opaque pixels become particle targets. Open the HUD to watch it work live — and to detune the morph constant until it feels wrong. Tuning is the difference between motion and noise.
